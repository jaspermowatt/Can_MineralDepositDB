Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) Airborne radiometric survey carried out in 1969 over the Hornby Bay basin by Aquitaine Company of Canada Limited discovered isolated mineralized sandstone outcrop 5 km east of Mountain Lake. Location of this 'original discovery' described by Bizard and Salat (1969) is given above. Two other showings were found about 850 m from it in direction 205° (at 116° 56' 51' W, 67° 16' 18' N) during the 1970 follow-up work that included a detailed helicopter-borne survey (Salat and Arnaud, 1970). In addition a number of radioactive boulders were also found in area of the company's PEC claim group. These showings and the boulders represent the first indication of uranium mineralization in the basal sandstone of the Dismal Lakes Group. Further exploration to the northeast, in the adjacent YUK claims, led to the discovery of gently dipping Mountain Lake deposit at a depth of 75 to 150 m, including overburden up to 60 m thick. The southwest end of the deposit is about 850 m to the northeast of the 'original discovery' showing. This and other showings occur in small areas of anomalous radioactivity. Two samples from the 'original discovery' showing assayed 1.4 and 1.6 % U (Bizard and Salat, 1969). Six samples taken in 1970 for multi-element analyses are reproduced in the 'sample data' section from Salat and Arnaud (1970). Trigg (1986, p. 299) suggested that these showings may once have been part of the Mountain Lake (PEC-YUK) deposit, the intervening part since eroded by glaciation. This is supported by some 4200 uraniferous boulders that form a westerly to northwesterly extending fan with its apex at the deposit. If the showings were once part of the deposit, the length of the deposit, prior to glaciation, was at least 2.4 km.
