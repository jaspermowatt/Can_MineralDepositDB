Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) This is one of many young uranium occurrences discovered by D.G.Leighton & Associates, Ltd. in the late 1970's. Work prior to the uranium moratorium in 1980 consisted of detailed auger sampling.

(Applies to: location and access) It is located in the Stinkhole Pond, approximately 10.5 km northwest of Summerland, B.C.

(Applies to: genetic model) The STINKHOLE prospect is a postglacial uranium concentration in lake-bottom sediments and surrounding marshes. The occurrence is recent, having formed from the interaction between uranium-rich groundwater and unconsolidated material containing organics or clay. This process is believed to still be taking place. The source of the uranium is thought to be the surrounding igneous rocks, where groundwater rich in carbonate and alkali ions have leached labile uranium from fresh rock exposed after glaciation.

(Applies to: reserve/resource descriptions) Uranium enrichment occurs over an area measuring 14,900 square metres. The STINKHOLE pond is approximately 60 metres by 150 metres and is set in a marsh which is almost 500 metres long by 100 metres at the widest point. Auguring of the STINKHOLE to an 8-metre depth revealed that the highest uranium content exists near the bottom (Assessment Report 6575). One hole averaged 0.0274% over 8 metres, with the bottom 3 metres averaging 0.0476% (Assessment Report 6575). A 1.0 metre section of another hole analyzed 0.0580% uranium (Assessment Report 6575). The marsh was augured to 1 metre depth, with the best uranium value being 0.0140 % (Assessment Report 6575). The STINKHOLE and surrounding marsh averages 0.0353 % uranium over a 5.2 metre thickness, beginning at 2.8 metres depth (Culbert, 1979). The highest 0.5 metre intersection analyzed 0.0984 % (Culbert,1979). Molybdenum enrichment (up to 0.07 %) also occurs (Culbert, 1988). An adjacent area to the southeast, informally known as the "Little Stink", occupies an area of 5,000 square metres and it averages 0.0212 % over 3.5 metres depth beginning at the surface (Culbert, 1979). The highest 0.5-metre intersection analyzed in the Little Stink was 0.0885 % uranium (Culbert, 1979).
