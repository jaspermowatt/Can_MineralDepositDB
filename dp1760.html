Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) This is one of many uranium occurrences discovered by D.G. Leighton & Associates Ltd. in the late 1970's.

(Applies to: genetic model) The NORTH FAULDER showing is a postglacial uranium concentration in a pond and associated marsh sediments. It is a surficial occurrence in postglacial unconsolidated material.

(Applies to: miscellaneous comments) The occurrence is recent, having formed from the interaction between uranium-rich groundwater and unconsolidated material containing organics or clay. This process is believed to be still taking place. The source of the uranium is thought to be the surrounding igneous rocks, where groundwater rich in carbonate and alkali ions have leached labile uranium from fresh rock exposed after glaciation

(Applies to: reserve/resource descriptions) Uranium enrichment occurs over an area measuring 20,000 square metres. The average thickness of the uraniferous layer is 2.5 metres, which averages 0.0349 % uranium (Culbert, 1979). Within that layer a 0.5 metre section averages 0.0606 % uranium (Culbert, 1979). The uraniferous layer lies 1.7 metres below the surface (Culbert, 1979).
