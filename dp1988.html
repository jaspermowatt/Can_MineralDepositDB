Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) Uranium mineralization in outcrops at the west end of Oora Lake was discovered in 1976 by Westmin crews. Follow-up exploration in 1977 included geological mapping, geophysical and geochemical surveys and testing of Showing 76-8 by 7 packsack drill holes. Airborne geophysical survey and regional geological mapping were done in 1978. Detailed geological mapping and magnetic survey were conducted in 1981 over a grid with a baseline 9 km long trending 075° and cross-lines every 50 m. The uranium mineralization occurs in feldspathic quartzite interbedded with argillite of the Showing Lake Formation along a synclinal keel zone. The host red beds are pyritiferous and contain about 5 % fine to medium grained magnetite. They have magnetic response of about 1100 gammas in contrast with the 400 to 600 gamma response over the underlying Oora Lake Formation of feldspathic quartzite lacking the argillite. Mineralization is exposed over a strike length of 1200 feet (365 m). The host beds dip 55° to the southeast. A grab sample of the mineralized magnetic redbed assayed 0.177 % U, < 0.005 % Th, and 0.02 % Cu (Schindler, 1977, p. 27).

(Applies to: mineralization) The Oora Lake Showing is hosted by the sandstones interbedded with siltstone-mudstone of the Showing Lake Formation (Young, 1980, report with maps, in Nutter, 1980). It overlies the feldspathic sandstone of the Oora Lake Formation, which serves as a stratigraphic marker unit. The Showing Lake Formation varies from a hard black quartzite to a softer chloritic slate, and generally has anomalously high uranium content (~ 50 ppm). Pyrite and copper sulphides (0.5 to 3 %) are common in this unit, and there seems to be a direct relationship between the sulphides and uranium contents. Magnetite is associated with the highest grade mineralization in bedrock at Showing 76-8, where it constitutes 10 % of the rock, and is also common in most of the uraniferous boulders found in the area. Lithologically the host beds are calcareous arenite. Numerous 'S' shaped voids occur on outcrop surface which were originally filled with calcite. The beds exhibit cross-bedding in some places indicating deposition along tidal channels in tidal flat environment. Drilling showed values of close to 0.3 % U3O8 over 2 to 4 feet in all the holes. The highest grade intersection was 2.7 % U3O8 over 1 foot. A second radioactive horizon occurs at 130 m from the main zone of Showing 76-8, and it is 130 to the north and 130 m to the west. This horizon contains much coarser grained rock. The host formation dominated by siltstone is about 200 m thick. The basal part of it has dark green siltstone to fine grained sandstone with micaceous matrix and phyllitic partings. Geochemical study (Knox, 1980, p. 157) shows that the uranium content of mineralized samples correlates to varying degrees with Pb, Mo, Cu, As, Bi, Co, V, S and Se, a dominantly chalcophile association. Magnetite is regarded as chemically precipitated in the host beds as it is finely distributed in intergranular spaces and locally as granules. Hematization of magnetite is common in mineralized zones. pitchblende grains commonly range in size from 1 to 5 microns, and coarser grains occur in high grade samples. Methane and hydrogen sulphide gas are invoked as reducing agents for precipitation of uranium during diagenesis of the host sediments that were relatively more porous. Paragenetically, magnetite was early, followed by hematite and then by pyrite and sulphides of chalcophile elements.
