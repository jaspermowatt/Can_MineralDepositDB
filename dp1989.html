Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) This showing is located 800 m west of the northwest corner of a lake referred to as the 'Big Lake' by Western Mines Limited (note: this name is also used for a lake in NTS 66 G 2 by Urangesellschaft Canada Ltd.). The radioactive horizon is on the margin of an outcrop 300 x 200 m (Knox, 1980, p. 119). It is a red fine grained sandstone bed 1 to 2 m thick. Maximum radioactivity on it is 82000 cpm on McPhar TV-1A spectrometer (equivalent to 1500 cps on SRAT SPP2 scintillometer). Average background values range from 1500 cpm on quartzite of Ayagaq Formation to 6500 cpm on siltstones of Showing Lake Formation. A grab sample from the radioactive horizon assayed 0.162 % U3O8. A chip sample across bedding over 7.5 feet averaged 0.69 lb U308/short ton (0.03 % U; Schindler, 1977, p. 25) . An angular large boulder at northeast margin of the outcrop, apparently derived locally, is of pink sandstone with a dark green siltstone bed on its underside that gave a reading of 100000 cpm. Bedding on the outcrop strikes 086° and dips 78° to the south toward the axis of an open syncline. Stratigraphically lower beds (northernmost) comprise fine grained pinkish to light brown sandstone with thinner interbeds of dark green micaceous siltstone. Proportion of siltstone increases to the south (up the section). The rhythmic bedding is characteristic of the southern half of the outcrop. This interbedding is typical of the 'tidalites', which is a characteristic feature of the Showing Lake Formation. Immediately north of the outcrop there are abundant large angular blocks of highly calcareous feldspathic sandstone belonging to older Oora Lake Formation. Hence the radioactive horizon is within a few tens of metres of the base of the Showing Lake Formation.
