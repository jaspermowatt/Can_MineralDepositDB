Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) A regional airborne gamma spectrometric survey conducted in 1978 by the Geological Survey of Canada located a uranium anomaly at Sandybeach Lake. Follow-up ground work in 1982 discovered mineralized boulders with high contents of U and significant amounts of other metals including Au, Co, Mo and W (Charbonneau and Swettenham, 1986). In 1987 Claude Resources Limited staked a claim (JAN-1) approximately 8 sq km in area around the anomaly, and carried out surface exploration intermittently until 1998, in part with Golden Band Resources Limited (Young, 1989; McGowan, 1993; Lehnert-Thiel, 1996). Adjacent claims were explored by Athlone Resources Limited (Sanguinetti, 1998). The Jan-1 claim was restaked in 1998 as LES-1 by Cameco Corporation, who carried out surface exploration during 1998 and 1999 that included glacial and petrographic studies on mineralized boulders (Gandhi and Charbonneau, 1998; Wasyliuk, 1999; Klassen, 1999, Madore and Annesley, 1999; Halaburda, 2000). The work to date has located more than 100 mineralized boulders, ranging in length from a few tens of centimetres to a metre or more, in a corridor 500 x 1000 m trending NNE in the region of scarce outcrops. It also showed that nearly half of these boulders are concentrated in 3 clusters viz., the Darcy's cluster at the location given above, and the Crescent Creek and Brian's Bay clusters about 300 m to the SE and 500 m to the S of it, respectively. The best assays obtained are 66.89 oz/t Au and 9.58 % U3O8 (8.124 % U) from samples in the Darcy's cluster. The glacial studies indicated that the boulders are locally derived. During 2002 ground magnetic, horizontal loop electromagnetic and induced polarization surveys were carried out over a grid 2.5 km long and 1 to 1.5 km wide (Ostapovitch, 2002). The surveys defined several drill targets (Charbonneau and Gandhi, 2002).

(Applies to: mineralization) The mineralization seen in the boulders at Sandybeach Lake is complex in terms of various metals and minerals. Some of the metals are granophile and others are of mafic magmatic affinity. Some features suggest synsedimentary concentration of Cu, Co, Ni, Mo and possibly of Au and U. Skarn-type mineralization is indicated by W, Au and albitization of the boulders. Hydrothermal mineralization or remobilization is apparent from the late stage veinlets carrying U, Au and other minerals. The area is mostly covered by glacial overburden. Bedrock sources of the mineralized boulders are not yet encountered. The glacial studies however indicate that the boulders are locally derived.

(Applies to: regional geology) The region straddling the Nunavut-Manitoba border was mapped by Eade (1971, 1973) on the north side of border and by Weber (1975) on the south side. They provided a regional picture of the Paleoproterozoic Hurwitz Group, intruded by granites related to the Late Paleoproterozoic Trans-Hudson Orogen. The metasediments are situated along the northwest boundary of the orogen that is well-defined by the boundary of the Wollaston Group in Saskatchewan (Money et al., 1970; Hoffman, 1980; Anonymous, 2003). The Hurwitz Group is intracratonic, and is distinct from the platform-shelf sequence of the orogen, viz., the Wollaston Group. Depositional history of the two sequences overlapped; the Hurwitz Group was deposited during 2.45-1.84 Ga, with a distinct break ca. 2.11-1.91 Ga (Bell, 1970; Aspler and Chiarenzelli, 1997), which separates the group into the lower and upper sequences. The lower sequence was deposited on the Archean Hearne Craton in relatively shallow waters in a large region of low relief. The upper sequence was deposited in relatively deeper water, mostly under the wave-base (Aspler et al., 2001). The Wollaston Group was deposited during 2.07-1.88 Ga, and was deformed, metamorphosed and intruded by granites of ca. 1.88-1.82 Ga Hudson Suite and of ca. 1.75 Ga Nueltin Suite (Anonymous, 2003; van Breemen et al., 2005). The Hurwitz Group was also affected similarly by the orogeny, in particular the metasiltstone-calc-silicate strata that host polymetallic occurrences in a 110 km long zone extending southwest from Sandybeach Lake (Schledewitz, 1980; Lehnert-Thiel, 1996). These strata are lithologically comparable with the Ameto and/or Watterson Formations, which are the lower two units of the Upper Hurwitz Group.

(Applies to: property geology) Host metasediments and granitoid rocks are albitized to varying degrees; some sulphide deposition was likely synsedimentary, which was superimposed by synmetamorphic and/or hydrothermal mineralization. Host strata are part of a belt that extends for 110 km SW of Sandybeach Lake and hosts several polymetallic prospects in Manitoba, including small Gillander Co-Au and Denison Co deposits at its SW end.
