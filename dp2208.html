Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) Location reported from two other databases is not accurate, but it falls close to northeast boundary of the large area covered by Canico-Suncor Joint Venture. It is 1 km east of the Slingshot Lake where detailed ground exploration was conducted in 1981 and 1982. The exploration project started in 1980 with helicopter-borne radiometric survey totalling 4525 line kilometres and Questor Input AEM surveying totalling 2190 km line kilometres. A total of 49 strong to very weak radiometric anomalies and numerous Input anomalies were reported. Preliminary ground check indicated at least 15 radiometric anomalies of interest. Exploration in 1981 included ground follow-up of airborne radiometric anomalies and Input responses, regional geochemical lake bottom sediment survey, reconnaissance and detailed HL and VLF EM surveys, water sampling for He and U, detailed alpha card, soil geochemical and spectrometer surveys. In addition detailed geological mapping in areas of outcrop and also prospecting in the claim area. The work delineated three principal target areas in claims TM-3, TM-34 (Dop Lake) and TM-16 (Slingshot Lake). The Slingshot Lake area is the site of three airborne radiometric anomalies traceable to numerous anomalously radioactive sandstone boulders, some of which may be frost-heaved. Alpha card anomalies further supported the anomalies. Further work in 1982 on the three areas included detailed lake sediment and water surveys, prospecting, till sampling, and alpha card, refraction seismic, gravity, HL and VLF EM surveys. The seismic soundings throughout the claim groups indicated depth to the bedrock greater than 300 m, with the sole exception of the Slingshot Lake area where the depth may be more than 100 m. Hence the radiometric anomalies are due to the overburden material. A 1.5 m deep pit was dug at the highest radiometric spot, and it was found that the alpha counts decreased with depth, hence the anomaly is within the till at less than 1 m depth. The radioactive boulders are transported by ice from the area to the southeast.
