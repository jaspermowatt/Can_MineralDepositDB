Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) - In 1926 Smuggler Hill Development Company was formed to explore and develop silver and lead deposits (Smuggler, 082M 023 and Foghorn, 082M 029), which were originally staked in 1918 by A.G. McDonald. The results of this early exploration activity were reported by H.G. Nicol, 1926 and D.B. Starrett, 1930. A manganese occurrence was examined by W. Elliot and N.C. Stines in 1929 (Smuggler Manganese, 082M 158). Further geological examinations of fluorite occurrences were reported on by D.B. Starrett, R.P.D. Graham and M.R. Wilson in the early 1940's (Spar, 082M 007). The ground was restaked in 1942 by Ole Johnson, and the B.C. Fluorspar Syndicate explored the fluorite deposit in 1943. The property was leased by A.E. Sjoquist and optioned in 1951 by Technical Mine Consultants who conducted an extensive exploration and development program for Rexspar Uranium and Metals Mining Co. Ltd. - The presence of uranium mineralization became known in late 1949. Dr. F.R. Joubin studied and reported on the mineral occurrences during 1950 and 1951. Rexspar Uranium, later reorganized as Consolidated Rexspar Minerals and Chemicals Ltd., acquired the rights to mineral claims incorporating the uranium bearing zones and delineated three uranium deposits in the late 1950's. However, the deposits were not brought into production. Denison Mines Ltd. resampled and undertook an economic feasibility study in 1969. Exploration programs and geological reviews were conducted in 1969-1970, mainly to determine the fluorite resources, which were estimated in 1971. Significant amounts of Mo, Sr, Ca, La, Yb and Y are found in the Fluorite zone. - Additional diamond drilling of the uranium bearing zones was carried out in 1976 and the drill core was used in a metallurgical test program undertaken to establish process flowsheets. In 1977 Kilborn Engineering (B.C.) Ltd. calculated uranium resources in the A, B and BD (Black Diamond) zones for Consolidated Rexspar Minerals and Chemicals Ltd. The A zone is relatively larger and somewhat higher in grade than the B and BD zones. These three zones and the Fluorite zone are located within a 1200x800 m area near surface, at elevation of about 1200 m. They are open-pittable. Several other similar but smaller occurrences of uranium and/or fluorite are found in the trachytes underlying the Foghorn-Lute creeks area.

(Applies to: regional geology) The Omineca Crystalline Belt is one of the five main geological provinces of the Canadian Cordillera south of 65° N. These are, from east to west, the Rocky Mountain (Foreland), Omineca, Intermontane, Coast Plutonic and Insular Belts. Much of the Cordillera west of Rocky Mountain Belt comprises complex accreted and suspect terranes that were emplaced during the Mesozoic. The region of Rexspar deposits is underlain by the Eagle Bay Formation of the Mount Ida Group. The formation includes a 200 m thick, gently dipping sequence of intermediate to acid volcanic rocks, which represent a volcanic centre. They host several uranium and fluorite deposits in a 4x3 km area between Foghorn and Lute creeks. The Eagle Bay Formation also includes green chlorite and chlorite-sericite schists, derived mainly from volcanic rocks. It is in fault contact on the west side with the Fennel Formation of the Slide Mountain Group, which comprises ocean floor rocks of Paleozoic (Mississippian?) age. The Eagle Bay assemblage extends more than 40 km to the south and southeast. It is intruded by the Cretaceous Baldy batholith, the north boundary of which is 6 km south of the Rexspar deposits. South of the batholith the assemblage includes intermediate to acid volcanic rocks representing additional volcanic centres. The southernmost volcanic suite is intruded by mid-Devonian Mount Fowler granitoid gneiss, which is dated at 372 ± 6 Ma by U/Pb zircon method. Accordingly at least part of the volcanic rocks in the formation must be pre-Middle Devonian. Conodonts from the highly deformed limestone and calcareous argillite indicate that the Eagle Bay Formation is in part Mississippian, and is likely correlative of the Slide Mountain Group. The formation as a whole is a complex and stratigraphically diverse succession ranging in age from mid-Paleozoic to Late Triassic, and has undergone post-Triassic deformation (Columbian orogeny) that developed the Shuswap Metamorphic Complex.

(Applies to: regional structure) Mesoscopic structural features are well exposed at a few key localities along Highway 5 on which town of Clearwater is located. The prominent schistosity, which is parallel to the compositional layering, was probably produced during the first phase of deformation. It is deformed by tight, recumbent, east-trending second-phase folds. These structures are refolded by upright third-phase, northerly to northeasterly trending structures. Subsequent late kinks and prominent north-trending tension fractures are commonly followed by post-tectonic felsic and mafic dykes of Cretaceous or later age. High-angle, northerly trending faults sharply control the distribution of the trachyte unit.

(Applies to: mineralization) The mineralized zones are subparallel to the host units, which are northeast striking, moderately northwest dipping felsic to intermediate metavolcanics and minor interlayered metasediments. Uranium and thorium mineralization occurs exclusively in the trachyte unit and mainly in its dark-coloured upper part, which shows extensive replacement by silver-grey fluorphlogopite and pyrite, with lesser fluorite and calcite. Drilling shows that ore-grade material occurs in a series of discontinuous lenses generally less than 20 m thick and as much as 140 m long. They are conformable with schistosity in the trachyte host. Fluorphlogopite and pyrite form coarse grained aggregates, which show conformable and cross-cutting relations with the host rock.

(Applies to: genetic model) The geological setting and mineralogy suggest that the mineralized zones were formed by deuteric, volatile-rich fluids during the evolution of a highly differentiated intrusive-extrusive suite (Preto, 1978). The considerable amount of thorium and widespread rare earths associated with the uranium support its origin as primary rather than secondary. A fluid inclusion study (Morton et al., 1978) showed that uranium (together with thorium and rare earth elements) was transported as carbonate complexes in a weakly saline aqueous system charged with CO2 of volcanic origin. During cooling at about 200° C the solutions were vented through fissures, resulting in sudden pressure drop and release of CO2 gas. Such a drop in partial pressure of CO2 likely caused precipitation of uranium minerals as the uranyl carbonate complexes would no longer be stable. In contrast, the Fluorite zone may have formed at a different time as it shows no evidence of the presence of CO2. It was probably formed from aqueous hydrothermal system during late stage incursion of meteoric waters. This would explain its relatively very low uranium content.
