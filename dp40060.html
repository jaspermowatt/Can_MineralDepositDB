Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) This was the first uranium mine in Canada, initially discovered in 1900 during regional geological mapping by J. M. Bell (1902), who noted 'cobalt bloom and copper stain'. In 1930 Gilbert LaBine, then President of Eldorado Gold Mining Company, and E. C. St. Paul discovered high grade silver-pitchblende veins. The veins were first mined for radium and silver from 1933 to 1940, when it was shut down because of World War II. Total radium production was 450 grams. Later advances in artificial production of radio-isotopes eliminated need for natural radium. The mine was reopened in 1942, under great secrecy during the war, at the request of governments of Great Britain and United States, because the latter was by then engaged in the Manhattan Project that needed uranium. In 1944 Canadian Government took over the property as a security measure and its name was changed to Eldorado Mining and Refining Limited. It continued production until 1960, which totalled 5773 t U and 1.5 million ounces of Ag (Jory 1964, p. 213-214). Average grade of the ore was 0.587 % U. The underground mine had reached a depth of 511 m, had 13 levels with total 41350 m of drifts, and13641 m of raises. By-products were Cu, Co, Ni and Pb. The ore was mined from veins numbered 1 to 8 from south to north and the Bear Bay Shear further north. Most of the production came from No. 1 shaft located in the middle of western half of the vein system, with minor contribution from two other shafts to the northeast. Veins 1, 2 and 3 accounted for 89 % of the total 983383 tonnes of uranium ore. In 1966 the mill and the surface plants were sold to the Echo Bay Mines Ltd., the company that mined silver from the veins on the adjacent property to the east, namely the Echo Bay Group of claims located approximately 2 km ENE of the Port Radium Shaft No. 1. This group of claims was staked in 1930 for the Consolidated Mining and Smelting Company Ltd. It was explored by diamond drilling in 1932 and two parallel adits were developed in 1934. The company carried out some more exploration 1948. In 1963 Northwest Explorers optioned the property and drilled 11 holes. Then Echo Bay Mines was incorporated and acquired the claims, and started production of silver and copper in 1964 utilizing the mill and surface plants acquired from the Eldorado Mining and Refining Company in 1966. By 1972 the Echo Bay mine had 6 levels and the mill was operating at 90 tonnes per day. In 1974 the company reached an agreement with Eldorado Mining and Refining Limited to exploit the silver ore from the Eldorado mine, and reopened the mine in 1976 and operated until 1982. A total of 11 million ounces of silver was produced from the silver-rich parts of same vein system that contained too little uranium for its economic recovery. The Eldorado (Port Radium) vein system during 1942-1982 produced total of 37 million ounces of silver and 10.5 million pounds of copper. Several other vein systems rich in silver but poor in uranium occur in the Echo Bay-Camsell River mining district, including the Terra silver mine located 56 km to the south that produced 28 million ounces of the metal.

(Applies to: structure) The Eldorado veins occur in a fault system within a NE-trending, lenticular area about 3.30 km long and up to 0.75 km wide (Fig. ), It is part of the regional northeast-trending steep brittle faults that affected the whole GBmz. The lenticular area of subsidiary faults in Port Radium is however rather unique. It is bounded to the northwest by the main Bear Bay Shear, which extends well beyond the Port Radium area. Other faults are subsidiary to it and are confined to the Port Radium area. All the faults dip steeply to the northwest. The host fault system is part of the regional northeast-trending brittle faults that affected the whole GBmz. The lenticular area of subsidiary faults is however rather unique. All these faults are filled with quartz-hematite veins that are common in the brittle faults throughout the GBmz. In the Echo bay-Camsell River mining district they are cut by the Cleaver dykes dated at 1740 +5/-4 Ma. The polymetallic mineralization postdates the Cleaver dyke as seen from the plan, cross-section and longitudinal section of the No. 3 vein (Jory, 1964). The No. 1 vein to its south extends towards the Echo Bay silver vein system 2 km east of the Eldorado No. 1 Shaft. A southwest branch from it extending to the Cobalt Island is aptly named Silver Island Vein. Thus the silver-rich and uranium-poor veins of the Echo Bay system and Silver Island vein are on the southeast side of the uranium-rich veins. It is apparent that polymetallic mineralization required reactivation of movements along the brittle faults to provide access to mineralizing solutions, and is attributed to tectonic activity west of the north-trending Great Bear magmatic zone (Gandhi and van Breeman, 2005; Hildebrand et al., 2009) and faulting that in part controlled deposition of the lower Hornby Bay Group, which unconformably overlies the older Cleaver dykes and is cut by Western channel diabase dykes and sheets dated at 1592 ± 3 Ma. Furthermore it includes the Narakay Islands intermediate-felsic volcanic suite that is dated at 1663 ± 8 Ma. In view of these ages the 1445 ± 20 Ma age obtained for pitchblende in Eldorado mine by Jory (1964) is somewhat younger, which is likely due to loss of radiogenic lead and/or redeposition of pitchblende.

(Applies to: mineralization) Successive reopening of the fracture zones during mineralization is evident. At least five stages of vein deposition are identifiable in paragenetic sequence as follows: 1: Hematite, quartz. 2: Pitchblende, quartz, hematite 3: Quartz, Co-Ni-Arsenides. 4: Co-sulphides, chlorite, dolomite. 5: Carbonate, Ag, Bi, chalcopyrite. About 40 metallic minerals and 9 non-metallic gangue minerals were recognized in the veins (Lang, 1962). The silver suite only occurs in the vertical members of the vein zone system, the Silver Island vein and veins 2A, 3, 4, 5. It forms economic deposits only in the first two of these. Pitchblende occurs in exceptionally rich concentrations, with some specimens that contain over 60 per cent U3O8. It occurs in following forms: 1: Colloform seams up to 1 inch thick. 2: Massive veins and lenses up to 2 feet in width. 3: Strung out pods and lenses in massive shears. 4: Discrete massive patches in barren veins. 5: Zones of fragments in quartz, chlorite or sulphides. 6: Brecciated, isolated fragments of quartz or wall-rock. 7: Rims around earlier fragments of quartz or wall-rock. Some of these types are intimately associated with quartz, forming what is called siliceous pitchblende ore.
