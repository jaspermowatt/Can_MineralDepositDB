Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) The first uranium airborne and ground prospecting in the area surrounding this prospect was undertaken in late 1960, and covered the region from Bissett Lake to the eastern end of Baker Lake. Radioactivity was discovered in the Paleoproterozoic rocks of Baker Lake basin overlying the Archean basement. Further exploration in 1974 included airborne radiometric and magnetic surveys, and ground follow-up of strong airborne anomalies. This work located the promising 74-1E and 74-1W showings in the Kazan Formation. During 1975-1976 the showings were investigated further by the Cominco-Pan Ocean Joint Venture. The work included ground IP, resistivity, magnetometer and VLF-EM surveys, lake water and soil geochemical surveys, geological mapping on scale 1 : 10000, and diamond drilling totalling 1413 m in 17 holes during 1975, and 1373 m in 11 holes during 1976. The drilling tested a strike length of 700 m. The best drill hole was KZ-10, which intersected mineralization at 3 intervals along a 120 metre length, including 0.84 % U3O8 (0.71 % U) over 5.4 m at about 110 m depth.

(Applies to: mineralization) Epigenetic U-Cu-Ag mineralization occurs as impregnations and fracture-fillings in sandstone within thermal aureole of dyke or small intrusion. It is termed 'Kazan-type' in the Baker Lake region. The intrusion in this area is generally lamprophyric dykes believed to be feeder dykes of the ultrapotassic lavas of the Christopher Island Formation overlying the Kazan Formation. Mineralized zone at 74-1E is roughly parallel to one of the dykes. Deposition of uranium is apparently controlled by redox reactions. Distinctive white, grey and red alteration zones are developed in the host sandstone. Miller (1980, p. 41-45) interpreted the mineralization as two stage process: the initial iron and copper sulphide mineralization occurred during the intrusive activity, and at later stage the sulphides reacted with oxidizing uranium-bearing ground waters circulating through permeable giant cross-bedded arkosic sandstone. The alkaline lavas and related intrusions are regarded as the source of uranium. Reactions with the sulphides involving carbonate ions and iron-oxides would precipitate uranium in highly oxidized hematitic red zone where native copper is also formed and other elements, mainly silver, vanadium and molybdenum, are scavenged by hematite.

(Applies to: diamond drill hole descriptions) The prospect is tested by 9 drill holes over strike length of 400 m; mineralization is discontinuous; the best grades are in red altered zones in arkose, which contain 0.5-3.5 % Cu and up to 0.25 % U. Less than 100 metric tonnes of contained U is estimated.
