Qualified comments: (Applies to: mineralization) Mineralization at Hawk Lake deposit has metallic signature comparable with the 'Kazan-type' sandstone-hosted occurrences in the Baker Lake basin, but is hosted in a breccia zone that is pervasively chloritized. The timing of brecciation is not certain. If it is related to volcanic hydrothermal activity then it would be coeval with the ultrapotassic volcanism of the Christopher Island Formation, and the mineralization likely accompanied the brecciation. If however the brecciation is related to the major northwest-trending brittle fault it would postdate the volcanism, because the fault has down dropped the Christopher Island Formation against the basement. Hence the mineralization would have to be younger as well. Minor quartz veining in the breccia zone is interpreted as pre-alteration and mineralization. Chloritization is common in the 'Kazan-type' occurrences but is not as pervasive as at Hawk Lake. It is likely that the brecciation facilitated the pervasive chloritic alteration. In this situation it would be difficult to discern a 'redox front', which is well developed in the 'Kazan-type' occurrences. Brecciation is neither common nor extensive in the 'Kazan-type' occurrences. For these reasons the Hawk Lake deposit is not regarded here as sandstone-hosted, and is treated as classical vein-type that includes breccia zones.

(Applies to: discovery and development) A group of 120 radioactive boulders was found in 1978. Surface work and drilling during 1978-1982 outlined the deposit nearby. 37 holes totalling 1787 m were drilled; the best result: 0.205% U3O8 over 23 m.
