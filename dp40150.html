Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) Follow-up ground work on an airborne radiometric anomaly (R 75) from survey by Kenting Geophysics and the regional geochemical anomalies (Evoy and Volz, 1981) resulted in discovery of a significant number of radioactive boulders. Seventeen boulders were assayed, which yielded up to 1.04 % U with low (under 0.5% P2O5 in bleached sandstone, and 200 to 1000 ppm U in red phosphatic sandstone with up to 14.9 % P2O5 (equivalent of 35% apatite). In 1982 a brief detailed prospecting work was carried out, which defined a narrow boulder fan or train (Hattie, 1982), and detailed lake sediment and water surveys showed strong uranium and helium anomalies which supported the regional surveys (Tilsley, 1982). The area is underlain by flat-lying Thelon Formation, the thickness of which is not known because of paucity of geological and geophysical data for the area. The radioactive boulder train is about 475 m long between two ponds and trends 300° (Nicholls, 2005, p. 28-30). Boulders of this train are phosphatic sandstone. A parallel train of non-radioactive limonitic boulder is located 125 m to the southwest, and is offset about 250 m to the west. Concentrations of uranium in 3 non-phosphatic boulders, which are characterized by secondary uranium minerals like torbernite and autunite, ranged from 120 to 10400 ppm U. Diamond drilling and additional prospecting are recommended as further work on the property (Nicholls, 2005).
