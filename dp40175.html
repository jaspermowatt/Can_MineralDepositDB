Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) The Rah (RAH) prospect was discovered in 1976 by Cominco Ltd when following up a regional lake water geochemical survey which led to a massive granite outcrop with pitchblende veins on a peninsula of Rah Lake. It was explored with 7 trenches in 1976, 8 trenches in 1977, and one diamond drill hole in 1978. The trenches revealed 18 veins from 2 to 30 m in length and up to a cm in width within a 400 x 100 m area. The drill hole, inclined at 45° to the N, showed 5 mineralized intersections, the deepest one to 45 m. Veins trend easterly and steep dip steeply, but variations in attitude are common. Their proximity to the basal unconformity of the Hornby Bay Group is evident from the location on the S margin of a graben-like structure in which basal beds of the Hornby Bay Group and hematite-rich regolith are down-faulted, and from a sandstone dyke in granite about 300 m SW of them. Five exploratory holes were drilled in the graben-like structure over a distance of 5.5 km northeast from the Rah prospect, through the Hornby Bay Group sediments toward the basement exposed on the SE side in the structure, without intersecting significant mineralization. Veins at the Rah prospect resemble numerous others of the Great Bear magmatic zone, in which pitchblende is associated with copper sulphides, and one or more of the gangue minerals quartz, calcite, chlorite and hematite. They are however unusual in that they have complex mineralogy and significant contents of Au, Pt and Pd, up to 56, 39 and 48 ppm, rasp., and a variety of minerals containing these metals (Gandhi and Paktunc, 1989). It may be noted that detailed mineralogical studies to date have not found platinum minerals or platinum-bearing phases. The veins form a small open-pittable deposit. A preliminary estimate of its resource potential by the company in 1978 is approx.1.8 million tonnes averaging 0.1 % U. The property remained dormant till 1987 when it was restaked as the 'Corhill' property by Aber Resources Ltd in joint venture with Norgold Ltd, a subsidiary of Broken Hill North Ltd of Australia, and Cominco Ltd. This staking was based on the concept of uranium-precious metal association seen in the 'Coronation Hill' mine in Australia, although no direct comparison has been made between it and the Rah prospect. Surface exploration in the surrounding region, which included lithogeochemical sampling of 335 selected samples, located 3 occurrences in addition to the 8 located in 1970s by Cominco Ltd (Blackwell and Mosher, 1989). The most noteworthy of the new occurrences is the WET U-Au showing approximately 6 km due 305° from the Rah prospect.

(Applies to: mineralization) The fracture controlled mineralization at the Rah prospect is located very close to the basal unconformity of the Hornby Bay Group, which is characterized by lateritic paleoweathering. In this regard the unconformity resembles those of other Mesoproterozoic basins containing continental siliciclastic strata, namely the Athabasca and Thelon basins. At the Rah prospect the intense clay alteration typical of Unconformity-associated deposits is not seen. The U-Au association is comparable with that found in the Unconformity-associated D orebody at Cluff Lake and in the veins at Nicholson mine, both in northern Saskatchewan. It is also found in the several deposits in Late Paleoproterozoic rocks of South Alligator River Valley district of Northern Territory in Australia. Association of platinum group elements with U-Au appears less common. The well known example of it is the Coronation Mine in the Australian district (McKay and Miezitis, 2001, p.73-80), where Au-Pt-Pd mineralization is adjacent to but separate from the uranium-rich zones. Repeated mobilization and redeposition of the metals by circulating meteoric waters in tensional environment over long period, according to Gandhi and Paktunc (1989, p. 251) may have resulted in the bringing together these lithogeochemically diverse metals at the Rah prospect.
