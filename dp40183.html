Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) The Jaciar prospect was discovered in 1977 by Hudson's Bay Oil and Gas Company Limited during an airborne gamma-ray spectrometer survey (King and Panenka, 1977). It is located about 4 km south of Jaciar Lake. Other work during 1977 included regional lake sediment and water surveys and limited geological mapping. Further exploration in the region by the company in 1978 included detailed airborne and ground radiometric surveys, induced polarization and EM surveys, and continuation of the regional geochemical surveys initiated in 1977. The Jaciar prospect was explored by excavation of 16 trenches about 0.5 m deep. Eight of the trenches are on the main zone about 70 x5 m, six others are on a smaller zone 750 m to the ENE, and two trenches are in-between them. Individual veins are up to 0.5 m wide. Chip samples from the trenches showed mean values of 0.13 % U3O8 (0.11 % U) and 0.03 % Cu over 1 to 2.5 m widths. Highest values reported are 0.44 % U3O8 (0.37 % U), 0.18 % Cu, 28 g/t Ag and traces of Au. Multi-element analysis of a copper sulphide-rich grab sample from the west end of the prospect showed Au, Ag, Pt and Pd contents of 970, <500, 850 and 1100 ppb, respectively (Gandhi and Paktunc,1989 p.248). Sporadic mineralization is reported over the strike length of 1.5 km along the giant quartz vein.

(Applies to: mineralization) The giant quartz vein follows a fault subsidiary to the major fault that trends 040° to 045° and passes through the east end of Jaciar Lake approximately 4 km northeast of the showing. The subsidiary fault is on the south side of it and trends 055°. Another subsidiary fault just south of it trends 075°. These subsidiary faults meet the major fault about 2 km southwest of the showing. Displacement along the major fault is right lateral, and is in the order of 3 to 4 km (Hoffman, 1978). The subsidiary faults accommodate minor block movements, and have lateral displacements in the order of a few hundred metres. The vein zone is several meters wide and a few kilometres long. It comprises at least two and probably three stages of veining. The first one is composed of massive quartz veins, which trend about 065° thus closely parallel to the associated fault. It contains highly silicified and brecciated fragments of the country rock, and is virtually unmineralized. The second generation veins are also massive but smaller than the older veins, and crosscut them at 30 to 40°. They contain some pyrite, and are limonite stained. The third generation veins have coarse crystalline quartz, are up to 5 cm wide and contain abundant hematite. These veins are mineralized in some places. The best mineralization is found where massive quartz forms groundmass to brecciated and highly silicified andesitic tuffaceous rocks. It is seen mainly as yellow uranium and green copper stains along fractures. Hematite staining is ubiquitous and not strictly associated with the mineralization.
