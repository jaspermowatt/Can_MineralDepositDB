Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) The Jacques Lake prospect was discovered during 2004-'05 by Fronteer Development Group Inc. It is located 500 m northeast of the McLean uranium showing discovered in 1957 by prospector McLean working for British Newfoundland Exploration Ltd. The latter was explored further by the company (Krajewski and Turner, 1976; McClintock, 1978). The mineral exploration concession held by the company from the 1950's was surrendered in 1985. A large part of it was staked by Fronteer Development Group Inc. in 2003. Discovery of the Jacques Lake prospect resulted from ground follow-up of an airborne radiometric anomaly detected during 2004 survey. The anomaly is 5 km long and 0.5 km wide. The McLean and Jacques occurrences are along a stratigraphic zone trending northeast and dipping steeply to the southeast, which is regarded as 'favourable corridor'. They are situated on the shoulder of high ground about a kilometre southeast of a large lake. In earlier geological literature the lake is referred to as McLean Lake, but now it is referred to as Jacques Lake, the original name used by the local population. Bedrock exposure is poor in the area. Some radioactive outcrops and boulders are however found along, and in the vicinity of the two uranium occurrences. The Jacques lake prospect was initially tested in 2005 by seven widely spaced diamond drill holes. Further exploration was undertaken in partnership with Altius Minerals Corporation, under a new joint venture company, namely Aurora Energy Resources Inc. The work included drilling of 44 holes in 2006, which outlined the main mineralized zone and smaller footwall and hanging wall zones. The resource estimates based in this drilling were released in February 2007. Additional drilling was planned for 2007.

(Applies to: regional geology) The host felsic volcanic and volcaniclastic strata are part of a structural-stratigraphic zone, which is less than 5 km wide and extends for 30 km west-southwest to the Michelin deposit area. The dips are commonly 60 to 80° to the southeast. The stratigraphic zone is part of the Aillik (former Upper Aillik) Group, which is dated by U/Pb zircon method in the Michelin deposit area and the coastal area near Makkovik at ca. 1853 Ma (Schärer et al., 1988). Details of the stratigraphy are difficult to define because of rapid changes of units along strike and dip, and structural complexity. Folding is tight and the dips are steep to the southeast or overturned to the northwest (Bailey, 1979). In the vicinity of Jacques Lake and McLean occurrences the strata include laminated to thinly bedded siltstone and sandstone, fine grained and porphyritic rhyolite flows and ignimbrites, and polylithic and monolithic rhyolite breccia. They were deformed prior to the emplacement of the undeformed and aerially extensive quartz-feldspar porphyry that forms the White Bear Mountain suite east-northeast of the Jacques Lake prospect. A sample of it from the summit of the mountain located about 8 km in direction 080° from the prospect is dated at 1807 ± 2 Ma. It is coeval with the Makkovikian granites in the region and is regarded as product of a younger magmatic pulse (Schärer et al., 1988; Kerr et al., 1992).

(Applies to: reserve/resource descriptions) A total of 51 drill holes outlined the main zone and two subsidiary zones on the hanging wall and footwall sides. Resources to a depth of 105 m are amenable to open pit mining, and the deeper resources to a depth of 275 m will require underground mining (Fronteer Development Group, Press Release: February 13, 2007).
