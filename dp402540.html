Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) The R-75 airborne radiometric anomaly is centred on the area of Lease 3256, which is rectangular about 2.2 km in N-S direction and is variable in width averaging about 1.9 km because of slightly tilted sides. It is located in the region underlain by flat-lying sandstone of extensive Thelon Formation. Ground follow-up prospecting in 1981 resulted in discovery of a significant number of radioactive boulders (Evoy and Volz, 1981). Seventeen boulders were assayed, which yielded up to 1.04 % U in bleached sandstone boulders containing less than 0.5% P2O5, and 250 to 1000 ppm U in red phosphatic sandstone containing high P2O5 up to 14.9%. More detailed prospecting in 1982 defined two mineralized boulder fans at azimuth 300° (Hattie, 1983). The eastern fan comprises boulders of phosphatic sandstone, and the second one comprises mainly of non-phosphatic limonitic sandstone with secondary uranium minerals e.g., torbernite and autunite. The eastern fan is about 500 m long with a narrowest point on the shore of a small pond and the widest part, as much as 60 m, on the shore of another pond that masks its further extension. The origin of the second fan is 250 m to the west (265°) of the narrowest point of the eastern fan. The width and direction of the second fan are comparable with that of the first fan, but its length is shorter (about 310 m) as its further extension is also masked by the same pond that masks the first fan. Uranium concentration in the non-phosphatic boulders ranges from 120 to 10400 ppm U.

(Applies to: mineralization) Hattie (1983) noted that the radioactive boulders are frost-broken rubble and it is difficult to recognized their original shape and size. He recognized the following 4 boulder types: (A) Quartz sandstones, often with quartz clasts up to 1 cm, some with kaolin; mainly reddish-coloured with pink to dark purple red apatite as interstitial cement either throughout the rock or favouring certain beds that are commonly finer grained. In many cases the phosphate cement formed ovoid aggregates or concretions. The maximum P2O5 assay was 14.9 %, which is equivalent of 35 % apatite and approximates the maximum pore space available. In some boulders apatite cement is more abundant for 1 or 2 cm near joints. Some pieces have crystalline coatings of pink apatite and white goyazite (?) along joint planes and also along less regular and smooth fractures. Some of the rocks have 'leopard-spot' weathered surfaces, which are areas less than 1 cm in diameter where silica-cementing has been inhibited by early accumulation of hematite and apatite in sandstone matrix. These spots are surrounded by a harder, silica-cemented network. Some of them have disseminated specular hematite. Boulders of this type give low to moderate counts, less than 500 cps, on SRAT-SPP2 scintillometer. Some boulders of Type A have bleached cream, porous rims resembling Type C boulders and a few others how large ( up to 10 cm long) blotches of dark hematite, approaching the Type B boulders. (B) These boulders are quartz sandstones with variable interstitial kaolin, distinguished by up to 20 cm patches (remnants?) of dark brown to purple staining, surrounded by grey-white sandstone. Some purplish bands are parallel to bedding. Some examples have quartz granules. One boulder had clusters of hematite. Scintillometer counts are low to high, up to 2500 cps. Such variation also occurs within the boulders. Counts may be higher on the bleached parts or on the darker hematitic parts. Some boulders have cream, limonitic speckled zones on some edges, which resemble that in Type C boulders. The highest counts and the highest assay (1.04 % U) were on a boulder with only 0.53 % P2O5. Assay on another sample confirms the leucophosphite content (< 0.01 %) of this boulder type. (C) These sandstone boulders are cream. porous, generally limonite-speckled rocks, occasionally with secondary green uranium mineral (torbernite) in pores and cracks. They are the most radioactive with readings up to6000 cps on SRAT scintillometer on a 15 cm long clast. Cavities up to a few millimetres in diameter
