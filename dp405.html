Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) - The Michelin deposit was discovered in 1968 by ground follow-up of a helicopter-borne spectrometer survey conducted in 1967 by the Brinex-Urangesellschaft Joint Venture. The deposit is named after prospector Leslie Michelin of Northwest River in Labrador, who located the radioactive outcrops at the site of a strong anomaly detected by the survey. The exposures occur over of 45 m along a felsic volcanic unit trending east-northeast. They have disseminated mineralization with moderately high but consistent radioactivity over widths of 1.5 to 2.4 m. Detailed surface exploration during 1967-'68 included blasting of 7 trenches, boulder tracing, and radiometric and magnetic surveys. Magnetic signature of the mineralized zone is weak. Area north of the zone is covered by a swamp. To the south locally derived radioactive boulders indicate the presence of the zone. Trench samples yielded assays up to 0.260% U3O8 across 1.5 m. Diamond drilling was undertaken in 1969, and a total of 10196 m were drilled in 79 holes. First resource calculation, done on the basis on 1909 core samples, a cut-off grade of 0.0848 % U and 20% dilution, gave 2.83 million tonnes averaging 0.1289 % U over a 1200x135 m zone dipping 50 to 60° S. Radon and soil geochemical surveys, and geological mapping continued during 1970-'71, and included drilling of 1833 m in 13 holes during 1970. During 1974 further geological mapping and radioactive boulder tracing were carried out in the area around Michelin deposit. An underground exploration of the deposit was conducted in 1976 with a decline 192 m in length at the east end of the deposit to a depth of 30 m to reach the ore zone, which was followed along strike by a drift 400 m long, and cross cut was made in the middle section of the deposit. This was accompanied by detailed underground mapping, muck sampling, chip or face sampling and bulk sampling. In addition underground drilling was done at 15 m intervals, which provided additional samples. Assay results on all these samples indicated an overall potential of 7416 tons per vertical foot at a grade of 0.139% U3O8 (or 6728 tonnes averaging 0.1179 % U per 0.3048 m). In 1977 a feasibility study of mining the low grade Michelin deposit and the smaller but higher grade Kitts deposit was conducted by Brine, which visualized blending the ores from the two deposits for milling on a site on a tidewater site of Kaipokok Bay. In 1979 fill-in drilling of 65 holes totalling 3943 m was carried and a second feasibility study was done by Kilborn Engineering Limited for the Brinex-Urangesellschaft Joint Venture, as well as an environmental impact study was carried out by Beak Consultants. However in 1980 an Environmental Assessment Board appointed by provincial government recommended after a series of public hearings and investigations that the Kitts-Michelin uranium project not be permitted to go ahead because of inadequate disposal techniques for radioactive waste products. In the following years uranium market declined, and concessions held by Brinex expired. Early in 2003, with renewed interest in uranium, Altius Minerals acquired mineral exploration license for the Michelin deposit. The company joined forces with Fronteer Development and formed Aurora Energy to explore uranium in Labrador. The new company drilled 9 holes in 2005 and 35 holes in 1976 on the deposit, which further defined the Main zone, extended it to deeper level and outlined two hanging wall zones. Extension below the historic 250 m level is based on 7 widely spaced holes. A new resource estimate combining the historic data with the newly acquired assays, was done by Roscoe Postle Associates, and was released in February 2007 by Fronteer Development Group. It includes open pittable resources to a depth of 185 m, and deeper resources requiring underground mining. These resource figures are given in the 'resource/reserves descriptions' comments. The deposit is in Labrador Inuit land (Nunatsiavut).

(Applies to: regional geology) The Michelin deposit and a number of other uranium occurrences are hosted by the dominantly felsic volcanic Aillik (former Upper Aillik) Group dated at ~ 1853 Ma (Gandhi 1986; Schärer et al., 1988). The group comprises thick and extensive rhyolitic flows, tuffs, ignimbrites and associated mainly volcaniclastic sediments, including conglomerate, sandstone and siltstones and some basaltic flows. It is as much as 8 km thick and is folded in a north-northeast to north trending belt about 40 km wide and 100 km long between Kaipokok Bay and Big River. In the southwestern part of the belt around Michelin deposit the lack of marker horizon makes it difficult to decipher folding and faulting of the volcanic sequence. Moderately steep dips of the strata, shear zones and foliation to the south-southeast are common and indicate overturned folds and thrusts to the north. The sequence was intruded by syntectonic and post-tectonic granites during 1810-1780 Ma. This extrusive-intrusive complex constitutes the main part of the Makkovik Structural Province (Taylor, 1971) between the Nain and Churchill Provinces to the north and the Grenville Province to the south. The Upper Aillik Group is also intruded by the quartz monzonite-granodiorite plutons that are related to the major east-trending, ca. 1650 Ma old Trans-Labrador batholith a few kilometres south of the Michelin deposit. This intrusive relationship is exposed along the north side of Walker Lake 9 km west of Michelin deposit. Dating of zircons from a sample near the contact confirms the affiliation of the pluton with the batholith that represents the 1.7-1.5 Ga Labradorean orogeny. The batholith is cut by a large east-trending Michael gabbro dyke 5 km south of the deposit. The dyke and the host batholith are affected by the Grenvillian deformation ca. 1000 Ma ago. The rocks in the Michelin area however appear to be little affected by this episode, although some structural adjustments are likely to have occurred. The Makkovik Subprovince is part of the Paleoproterozoic accretionary belt along the south margin of Laurentia-Baltica craton, which extends northeast through the Ketilidian mobile belt of South Greenland to the Fennoscandian orogen of Sweden and Finland, and southwest to the Penokian orogen in Wisconsin (Gower et al., 1994). Marine seismic profiles across the Makkovikian-Ketilidian orogen are interpreted by Kerr et al. (1997) as indicative of a doubly vergent, asymmetric orogenic belt comparable in width to younger collisional orogens. They presented a multistage model that incorporated initial northward subduction beneath the craton, shifting to later southward subduction, followed by oblique accretion of a composite arc terrane and juvenile (?) continental block. A chronological review of events in the Makkovik orogen, presented by Ketchum et al. (2002), includes accretion of a Paleoproterozoic island arc at 1.9 Ga leading to deformation and amphibolite-facies metamorphism of the rift-drift assemblages (Moran and Post Hill groups) and the Archean foreland (Nain Province). Following this collision, craton-ward subduction formed the 1895-1870 Ma Island Harbour Bay Plutonic Suite, which is a calc-alkaline magmatic arc built on reworked Archean crust. The crust formation continued during 1860-1850 Ma with deposition of the Aillik Group in a rifted arc or back-arc setting. Sometime before 1802 ma the Aillik Group was thrusted over the reworked Archean crust, possibly accompanied by accretion of another arc to the south (Cape Harrison Metamorphic Suite). Regional transpression, amphibolite-facies metamorphism and widespread granitoid plutonism occurred during 1815-1780 Ma.

(Applies to: genetic model) The following features are cited by Gandhi (1978,1986) as pointing to the volcanogenic stratabound nature of the deposit: (i) the mineralized lenses are subconcordant to the host sequence of rhyolitic volcanics, which is in part subaerial, and are effected by deformation and minor displacements parallel to regional trend and foliation, (ii) uranium occurs mainly as disseminated fine granules less than 10 micrometres in diameter, often in association with finely disseminated hematite and accessory sphene, (iii) significant enrichment of soda and depletion of potash in the host rocks are associated with uranium mineralization, (iv) the mineralized zones are cut by barren mafic dykes that are metamorphosed to amphibolite, (v) local pitchblende veins are developed near small felsic and mafic intrusions that caused remobilization of uranium, (vi) geochronological data on pitchblende and radiogenic galena from the deposit indicate that the timing of mineralization close to the age of the host rocks viz., 1856±2 Ma, and reflect later isotopic disturbances due to deformation and intrusions, (vi) a number of uranium occurrences similar to but smaller than the Michelin deposit are found in the Upper Aillik Group, indicating that the mineralization is a regional phenomenon. Formation of volcanogenic uranium deposits involves an initial enrichment of the metal in the felsic magma source, and later concentration of it at the deposit site during and/or after the volcanism from the magmatic fluids/hydrothermal solutions or meteoric waters or a mixture of them (Gandhi and Bell, 1995). In case of the Michelin deposit opinions differ as to the relative importance of magmatic fluids and meteoric waters. Magmatic fluids enriched in U, Na and volatiles (CO2, F and Cl) at the waning stage of volcanism are favoured by Gandhi (1978, 1996). These migrated through permeable avenues in the volcanic sequence, reacted with Fe- and/or Ti-bearing minerals sparsely distributed in the host rocks and deposited uranium. This was accompanied by soda metasomatism of the host rock. Kontak (1980) advocated a combined role of meteoric water, which that leached uranium from devitrified glass shards and tuffs, and volatile-rich fluids emanating from vents. White and Martin (1980) visualized convection cells of mildly alkaline aqueous fluids of meteoric origin near intrusive plugs and inferred subjacent plutons related to volcanism, and deposition of uranium at temperature above greenschist facies. Gower et al. (1982) also postulated similar convection cells but thought the Upper Aillik Group as resting unconformably over deformed Lower Aillik Group at the time of mineralization. Evans (1980) on the hand invoked only the low temperature, neutral to weakly alkaline, oxidizing ground waters of meteoric and/or of connate marine origin, for leaching of U and Zr from the felsic volcanic source rocks under diagenetic to zeolite facies pressure-temperature conditions. Michelin deposit is the largest uranium deposit found to date in the orogen extending from Labrador through Greenland to the Baltic Shield. In the Svecofennian part of the orogen a number of disseminated and vein type uranium occurrences hosted by felsic volcanic strata have been found (Adamek and Wilson, 1975; Lindroos and Smellie, 1979; Gustafsson, 1981; Hålenius et al., 1986; Gower, 1992; Gandhi and Bell, 1996). Their geological setting, deposit characters and geochronological data are comparable with those of the uranium occurrences in the Aillik Group.

(Applies to: reserve/resource descriptions) The resources calculated by Roscoe Postle Associates were reported by Fronteer Development Group in a press release dated February 13, 2007. They are in the open pit and underground domains, based on approximate depths obtained from an on-going scoping study. The open pit resources extend to a depth of 185 vertical metres, and the underground resources extend for additional 400 metres below the base of the pit. The cut-off grades for them are 0.03% and O.05% U3O8 respectively, and the average density used is 2.71. Open pit resources include Measured 3410000 tonnes averaging 0.07% U3O8, Indicated 7930000 tonnes averaging 0.06% U3O8, and Inferred 460000 tonnes averaging 0.04% U3O8. Underground resources include Indicated 14310000 tonnes averaging 0.12% U3O8, and Inferred 13950000 tonnes averaging 0.11% U3O8.
