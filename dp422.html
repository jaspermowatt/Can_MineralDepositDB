Qualified comments: (Applies to: discovery and development) Kitts deposit was discovered in 1956 by prospector Walter Kitts working for British Newfoundland Corporation (Brinco), the company that developed the Churchill Falls hydro electric power and had mineral exploration concessions in the region. The first pitchblende discovery in the region was about 20 km to the east in 1954 by M.J Piloski of the company (Morrison, 1956, Beavan, 1958). Some grab samples from the Kitts showings assayed over 2 % U3O8. Trenching and drilling in 1957 confirmed the high grade of the mineralized zones. Further drilling later that year provided encouraging intersections, and a 171 m long adit was driven during the winter of 1957-'58 from the east side of the hill to reach the Kitts A and B zones, which strike north-northwest and dip steeply to the east. A total of 390 m of drifting from the adit, and 818 m of drilling in 78 underground holes were done to explore the two zones. In addition surface diamond drilling continued till September 1957 and totalled 7576 m in 76 holes. In April 1958 a bulk sample of 25 tons comprising ore grade and wall rock material was sent to the Mines Branch in Ottawa for mineralogical and metallurgical studies (Hughson, 1958a,b and c). The ore mineral was identified as pitchblende, and the grades ranged from 0.089 to 1.36 % U3O8. An acid leach extraction test showed uranium recovery of 95 %. Ore reserves were estimated by M.J. Piloski (1968) at 186000 tons averaging 0.883 % U3O8 (uncut) and 0.449 % U3O8 (cut), and a preliminary mine design study was also carried out. Brinco then formed a wholly owned subsidiary, British Newfoundland Exploration Ltd. (Brinex), to continue exploration and conduct a feasibility study for mine-mill complex to treat 250 tons per day. The uranium market however declined in 1959 as the United States stopped purchasing it from Canada beyond the amounts required in the initial contracts. The market rebounded in mid-1960s with the advent of nuclear power for civilian use. Brinex resumed exploration in 1964 in joint venture with Metallgesellschaft A. G. of Germany (later Urangesellschaft Canada Ltd.) in the Kaipokok Bay-Big River area. An airborne radiometric survey conducted over the area in 1967, which revealed a number of target zones along the southwesterly trend of the Kitts-Post Hill belt and the Michelin-White Bear Mountain belt to the south. Follow-up exploration brought to light several prospects and the larger but lower grade Michelin deposit located about 60 km to the southwest of Kitts deposit (Gandhi, 1978). By 1969 it was considered that the combination of the Michelin ore with the higher grade Kitts ore as 'sweetener' could make viable feed for a common mill located on seashore at the southwest end of Kaipokok Bay. In 1974 further exploration of the Kitts deposit was undertaken to firm up the reserves and find more. This involved drilling from surface and addition of a 330 m long underground decline from the end of the adit to a new level 38 m below the adit level. A drift and underground drill holes from the new level, and the surface drilling provided additional data for revision of the reserves. A preliminary feasibility study was conducted in 1976 by Kilborn Ltd. for the Joint Venture, and more detailed one was carried out during 1978-'79. The project visualized treating 1300 tons per day for 15 years. An Environment Impact Statement was presented in 1979 to the Environment Assessment Board, which recommended that the project not be allowed to proceed on the basis of inadequate provisions for waste disposal and long term monitoring, and inadequate studies of social and economic impacts. The concessions granted to Brinco expired in 1980s, and exploration in the region was resumed by other companies. The Kitts deposit however happened to be in the area of Mineral Exemption Land as claimed by the native community, and remained dormant.

(Applies to: mineralization) The Kitts deposit has two small tonnage high grade ore bodies. The deposit comprises A and B zones, which are hosted by an argillite unit about 20 m thick. Most of the uranium occurs as disseminated pitchblende grains and aggregates in en echelon lenses along the argillite unit. Pitchblende grains are about 5 microns in diameter, contain only traces of thorium and have a cell edge of 5.47 A. Within these essentially stratiform zones, the uranium mineralization is concentrated along certain thin beds and laminae of argillite, and to a lesser extent occurs in veins, along fractures and shears, and in matrix of local breccia zones that extend into the adjacent chert and basal flows, and in intrusive quartz feldspar porphyry. The A and B zones are located on the southwest limb of a tight syncline, interpreted on the basis of consistent stratigraphic sequence of lower basalt, chert, argillite and upper basalt, and top determinations on the two basalt units. Their strike length is about 200 m. The A zone dips about 50° to the northeast and plunges moderately to the southeast. The B zone southeast dips steely to the northeast. It is tightly folded and faulted in contrast with the A zone, and it has larger tonnage and higher grade ore. Higher concentrations of uranium commonly occur at hinges of folds and crenulations in argillite. Grades are highly variable and range up to 20% U3O8 in some places. Distribution of uranium is log normal, and systematic variations can only be expected up to 4 m from a sample site, beyond which it is random. The host argillite is fine grained and contains varying amounts of albite, quartz, amphibole, pyroxene, biotite, chlorite, graphite, calcite, pyrrhotite, pyrite and chalcopyrite. Traces of arsenopyrite, molybdenite, ilmenite and hematite have been noted. Amphibole is the main mafic silicate, and is normally green and clouded with inclusions of opaque minerals including pitchblende, but is reddish brown in highly radioactive spots. It appears to have grown later than other minerals. Chlorite is restricted to the sheared surfaces. Graphite content varies from 1 to 2.5 percent. Pyrrhotite occurs in amounts to up to 10 percent, and locally forms thin beds and lenses up to 5 mm thick. Where these are tightly folded the sulphides also form veinlets cutting surrounding rocks. Calcite occurs commonly as veinlets, and amounts to as much as 7 percent. Both white and pink varieties of calcite are present, the latter being typical of mineralized zones. Quartz-calcite veins carrying traces of pitchblende extend into the surrounding country rock.

(Applies to: genetic model) - Several genetic models have been proposed for the Kitts uranium deposit, and they have bearing on other uranium deposits along the Kitts-Post Hill belt. The models invoke syngenetic and various epigenetic processes. Each model tends to emphasize one or more aspects, but they all seem to fall short on explaining the complexity of features displayed by these deposits. More recent geochronological data and their structural implications have helped but not fully resolved the inherent problems of source, transportation and deposition of uranium. - Early observers emphasized the pitchblende veins and mineralized shears that are indicative of classical epigenetic uranium deposits (Beavan, 1958). Detailed stratigraphic study led Gandhi (1978, 1986) to emphasize the fact that most of the uranium occurs as disseminated grains in lenses along a graphitic sulphide-bearing argillite unit, which about 20 m thick. The reducing environment in which the original shaly beds of the unit were deposited is also favourable for deposition uranium. Pre-deformation mineralization is supported by folded mineralized lenses with high concentration of uranium at the fold hinges. Uranium is also smeared along some of the shears along the unit. Overgrowth of amphibole with numerous inclusions of pitchblende grains attests to the pre-metamorphic deposition of uranium. Later deformation and intrusions of gabbro and quartz feldspar porphyry caused local remobilization of uranium along fractures, but did not destroy the stratiform character. Source of uranium was postulated as the felsic volcanic activity to the south that formed the thick pile of Aillik Group. Coincidence of the U-Pb pitchblende ages and Rb-Sr ages of the felsic volcanics at ca. 1700 Ma supported this hypothesis of uranium derivation. Later geochronological studies have however shown the felsic volcanics were deposited ca. 1860 Ma. The pitchblende ages may be reconciled with these data by suggestion that the pitchblende ages reflect resetting during the Makkovikian orogeny. Marten (1977) and Evans (1980) on the other hand emphasized pre-mineralization deformation of the Kitts-Post Hill belt that produced permeable shear zones in the host units, and postulated that uranium derived from the felsic volcanic pile was deposited in these permeable zones. Marten (1977) visualized the mineralizing solutions to be metamorphic in origin, whereas Evans (1980) invoked low temperature, neutral to weakly alkaline, oxidizing ground waters of meteoric and/or connate marine origin for leaching of uranium from the source rocks and its transportation to the deposition sites. Gower et al. (1982) favoured essentially syngenetic mineralization in the felsic volcanics by circulating meteoric waters, with some contribution from connate and magmatic waters, but epigenetic mineralization in the Kitts deposit as the mineralizing solutions reached the base of the volcanic pile. Wilton and Wardle (1987) related the uranium mineralization in the Kitts-Post Hill belt with the intrusion of synkinematic granite during the Makkovikian deformation ca.1800 Ma. - Recent geochronological work showing the age of 2178 Ma for lower mafic volcanic unit at Post Hill (Ketchum et al., 2001) is permissive of significantly older age for the mafic volcanics at the Kitts deposit. Further work is however needed, to determine precise age and structural relations of the mafic volcanic units and associated sediments of the belt.
