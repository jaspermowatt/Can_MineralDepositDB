Qualified comments: (Applies to: location) The oblong dome formed by the caldera can be viewed in Google Earth. The imagery clearly shows a late NE-trending fault cutting through its core parallel to the long dimension. The pronounced dome is due to the extrusion of porphyroclastic rhyolite after the collapse of the caldera. It hosts at least 18 deposits have been found around the northern and western margins near major fault zones (Chemillac et al, 2005). Therefore, there has been no attempt to improve the general location supplied by the UDEPO database of the International Atomic Energy Agency.

(Applies to: mineralization) As quoted from Chemillac et al (2005): "Five types of deposit have been distinguished on the basis of mineral associations: U-REE association, U-Mo association, U-Pb-Zn-Ag association, U-P association, and U-Th-Mo-P-REE association."

(Applies to: regional geology) As reported by Chemillac et al (2005), the volcanics in the caldera belong to two cycles: Middle Jurassic felsic volcanics including rhyodacite and rhyolite tuffs, and Early Cretaceous porphyroclastic rhyolite which followed caldera collapse. Both cycles are geochemically distinct, and their melt inclusions are not very different from their whole rock compositions. The rocks of both the first and second cycles can be considered uranium sources, as can contributions from schists and phyllites in the basement. Though structurally-influenced, the distribution of the deposits appears to surround the second-cycle porphyroclastic rocks.

(Applies to: reserve/resource descriptions) Reserves were reported to exceed 26,000 metric tons at 0.1-0.3 % U (Chemillac et al. (2005).
