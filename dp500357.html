Qualified comments: (Applies to: miscellaneous comments) Google Earth shows what seems to be a morphological crater to the east of and near Oktyabrskiy. There is a pronounced open pit just east-southeast of Krasnokamensk, but this probably belongs to a past producer Krasny Kamen. There appears to be topographic highs that underlie both towns and locations, and coalesce in what might be a complex caldera setting sitting in the midst of a lowland. There are clearly many mine or mill buildings, tailings heaps and ponds in the area, one showing clear signs of bright colour, probably particulate matter. The IAEA states that this caldera complex hosts up to 20 individual deposits.

(Applies to: mineralization) The Streltsovka Caldera is known to have contained at least 280,000 t U3O8 in IAEA's 80 US$/kg cost category and 30,000 t U3O8 in the 130 US $/kg category, distributed in 20 known deposits. The main uranium mineralization was introduced well after the caldera was filled, during Stage IV of the alteration sequence, and consists predominantly of quartz, molybdenite, and pitchblende in the matrices of steeply dipping lensoid brecciated zones, and an Early Cretaceous date has been obtained in 1996 from pitchblende (Chabiron et al., 2003). Alteration began as albitization in the deep zones on the deposit, and was superseded by more widespread argillic alteration, followed by generally U-poor quartz veining. Chabiron et al. (2003) postulate that while the total amount of uranium found in the known deposits could, on the basis of mass-balance calculations using volumes and analyses of fresh melt inclusions and of the bulk of the rhyolite which is highly altered, have released by exsolution of the volatile phase from the extruded volcanics. However, based on the typically low KD of fluid/melt for U in such melts, they think that substantial input of hydrothermal fluid from the underlying magma chamber and possibly other sources. They also postulate that some uranium may have been leached from the potentially uraniferous Hercynian granite basement, explaining the anomalously high uranium resources of this caldera.
