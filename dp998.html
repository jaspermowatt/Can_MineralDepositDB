Qualified comments: (Applies to: location and access) The Blizzard uranium deposit is located 2.5 kms north of Lassie Lake and approximately 11.75 kms northwest of the Kettle Valley community of Christian Valley. Road access is via paved Highway 33 to Beaverdell, which is located about 20 km south-southwest of the deposit. A gravel road for 12 km from the deposit connects with the highway about the same distance north of Beaverdell.

(Applies to: discovery and development) The Blizzard uranium deposit was discovered by Lacana Mining Corporation in 1976 and explored until late 1970's. The Blizzard property was optioned from Lacana by Norcen Energy Resources Limited. A joint venture company comprised of Norcen, Campbell Chibougamou Mines Ltd., E&B Explorations Ltd., and Ontario Hydro completed 478 reverse circulation and diamond drill holes, totalling 21,184 m of drilling during 1976-1979 that outlined the deposit. It was the subject of positive feasibility study by Kilborn Engineering (B.C.) Ltd. in 1980. In February 1980 a 7-year moratorium on uranium exploration in British Columbia was imposed by the provincial government. It ended during a period of low uranium prices, and the Blizzard property claims and most other uranium claims in the province were allowed to lapse. The property was restaked in 2005, but came open soon after and was acquired by Adam R. Travis, who optioned it to Santoy Resources Ltd. and Spartan Resources Inc., under an agreement that allows the companies to acquire 100 % interest with option payments over a 4-year period. A review of the resources in the property for the companies was carried out by Christopher (2005), who recommended application of in situ leaching technology in view of the environmental concerns in the area.

(Applies to: regional geology) The area is underlain by granite and granodiorite of the Cretaceous-Tertiary Okanagan Batholith, and by quartz monzonite of an unnamed Middle Jurassic intrusion to the southeast. Metasediments of the Carboniferous-Permian Anarchist Group outcrop several kilometres to the southwest. These rocks were affected by Mesozoic compressive orogenies. The Miocene-Pliocene Chilcotin Group occurs as isolated, flat-lying, cap rocks consisting of vesicular and massive columnar olivine basalt with occasional intraformational sediments. A potassium/argon age of 5.1 plus or minus 0.50 Ma. was determined for the basalt (Geological Survey of Canada Open Files 1969). Miocene fluvial sediments underlying the basalts are unconsolidated, interbedded arkosic sandstones, siltstones, carbonaceous mudstones, and basal conglomerates. These sediments occur as structurally controlled 'paleochannels', which are host to uranium deposits.

(Applies to: property geology) The Blizzard deposit occurs along a sinuous southeast trending paleochannel, with a plunge of 1.5 degrees. Mineralization has been traced over a 1600 metre length, with widths from 60 to 265 metres and true thickness from 1 to 2.4 metres. The deposit is from 2 to 90 metres below surface. It is covered by plateau basalt except at the southern end. The basalt cover has a maximum thickness of 74 metres. The largest proportion of the uranium is concentrated in two ore zones, one in mudstone-sandstone beds and the other in sandstones immediately overlying basal conglomerate. At the northern end of the deposit uranium occurs within basal conglomerates and along the basalt-sandstone contact. A breccia body related to basalt extrusion occurs near the northwest end, and hosts some mineralization. The basement rocks to the fluvial sediments are mainly of Okanagan Batholith.

(Applies to: mineralization) Uranium mineralogy is represented by the uranyl and uraniferous phosphate minerals, saleeite, ningyoite, and autunite. Minor concentrations of pitchblende apparently replaces ningyoite pseudomorphically. Saleeite and ningyoite commonly cement carbonaceous rich quartzo-feldspathic sediment, whereas, ningyoite is the only ore mineral present in mudstone or at limonitised sandstone-mudstone interfaces. Autunite is confirmed to the basal sedimentary members and the northern part of the basement complex, occurring within fractures. Other minerals, including pyrite, which increases to the south of the deposit, marcasite, gypsum, rozenite, jarosite and trace sphalerite and carnotite.

(Applies to: genetic model) The deposit is interpreted as hydrogenic, formed by the concentration of uranium leached by ground water from nearby highly radioactive intrusive rocks in the basement, and deposited in conglomerate and sandstone along a paleostream channel. It is preserved from weathering and erosion by a cover of younger plateau basalts that extruded about 5 million years ago. Most of the uranium resides in phosphatic minerals, and minor amounts of zinc and vanadium are associated with it.

(Applies to: reserve/resource assays) Historical reserve estimate was made in August 1979 by Kilborn Engineering of British Columbia, which put the size of the deposit at 2,200,000 tonnes grading 0.214% U3O8 (0.182 % U). It was revised by Christopher (2005) using a cut-off grade of 0.025% U3O8 (0.021% U) over a 1-metre interval, as 'Indicated Resources' of 1,914,973 tonnes with average grade of 0.247% U3O8 (0.2095% U), and 'Inferred Resources' of 4,685 tonnes with average grade of 0.162% U3O8 (0.1374 % U). Contained uranium in the two categories are 4,728,428 kg U3O8 (400971 kg U) and 7595 kg U3O8 (6441 kg U), respectively.
